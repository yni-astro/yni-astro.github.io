Understanding the global evolution of protoplanetary disks (PPDs) is critical for setting the initial conditions for planet formation. However, existing theoretical modeling of PPD evolution has largely focused on isolated evolutionary phases, often employing idealized initial conditions and oversimplified prescriptions for angular momentum transport. To overcome these limitations, we present a semi-two-dimensional ($1+1$D) model to investigate the global evolution of PPDs with more realistic initial conditions and more detailed prescriptions for angular momentum transport. Our model incorporates key physical processes, including gravitational instability (GI), magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) winds, magneto-rotational instability (MRI), stellar irradiation, self-shadowing arising from disk geometry, and radiation transport. In our **Bz\_10mG** model, the interplay between disk geometry, GI, and MHD winds allows a long-lived, puffed-up inner disk to cast a deep shadow over the intermediate disk, sustaining a massive, gravitationally unstable, self-shadowed region for $\sim1\,\mathrm{Myr}$ and preserving a compact ($\lesssim10\,\mathrm{AU}$), cold ($\sim 10\,\mathrm{K}$), low-turbulence ($\alpha_\mathrm{SS}\sim 10^{-4}$), high-density ($\Sigma\gtrsim 300\,\mathrm{g}\cdot\mathrm{cm}^{-2}$) reservoir for several Myr. As wind-driven transport and associated gas depletion remove the outer disk, the remaining mass is concentrated in this optically thick reservoir, so disk masses inferred from mm continuum emission can substantially underestimate the absolute disk mass. In the contrasting **Betaz\_3e4** model, where $B_z$ scales with local gas pressure, wind-driven transport is strongest in the dense inner disk; depletion of the inner rim removes the global shadow and the disk evolves toward an extended, directly irradiated, flared configuration whose observable mass more closely traces the absolute mass. A parameter survey across different magnetic field configurations confirms the qualitative robustness of each evolutionary branch. Our results provide a physically consistent evolutionary pathway from GI-dominated Class 0/I disks to MHD wind-driven Class II disks, and point to three broader conclusions: (i) disk physics is strongly inhomogeneous in both space and time, so constant-$\alpha$ treatments miss essential physics; (ii) thermodynamics plays a significant role, with self-shadowing simultaneously preserving GI and weakening MHD winds; and (iii) the distribution of large-scale magnetic flux is the decisive unknown in disk evolution, as it controls whether the shadowed configuration can be maintained. The long-lived, low-turbulence, high-density shadowed or partially shielded regions we identify offer favorable conditions for dust grain growth and planetesimal formation, and our findings qualitatively align with recent observations of both compact/faint shadowed disks and more extended irradiated disks.
